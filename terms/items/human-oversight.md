Human oversight means a person can monitor an AI system, understand what it is doing, and step in if it starts to make a bad choice.

In practice, this can mean a person reviews outputs, watches for mistakes, approves risky actions, or stops the system if something looks wrong. Good oversight needs real information and real authority, not just a name in a process chart.

This matters because AI systems can miss context, make mistakes, or act too quickly for a person to catch later. Human oversight is one way to reduce harm when the result affects money, safety, rights, or other important decisions.

It is not the same as “we have a human somewhere in the process”. If the person cannot see enough detail, cannot act in time, or only rubber-stamps every step, then the oversight is weak. It is also not the same as human approval, which is just one specific yes-or-no checkpoint inside a bigger oversight setup.
